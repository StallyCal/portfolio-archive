Intent & Outlets
Statement of Purpose
I started building things before i had a name for what i was doing. By the time I was in highschool, i picked up guitar, keyboard, and flute, and started producing music on my own — first rock and metal, then hip-hop and RnB, and more recently an attempt to fold Persian and Arabic modal scales into contemporary production. None of it came from a class. i learned it by listening obsessively, then trying to recreate what I'd heard, badly at first, until it stopped being bad. The same instinct runs through most of what I make. I taught myself enough to build and code my own website from scratch, because I wanted a place that actually looked like the work inside it, not a template that happened to be holding it. When I came across an artist's optical-illusion drawing technique — building a moving form out of equidistant lines on a flat surface — I learned the method myself and used it to draw a piece for a café wall in Jodhpur. I'm not interested in keeping things I admire as references. I'd rather take them apart and see what I can build with the pieces. What connects the different things I make is less a philosophy and more a habit: I keep noticing what happens when you remove the obvious way of experiencing something. Im currently developing a music video built around a track I'm scoring myself — a vocal melody translated into cello, set in a room full of people watching a performance, except the one person moving through the crowd is blindfolded, navigating entirely by sound. It's less a statement and more a question I wanted to build and actually watch happen, instead of just thinking about it. Design to me, is the part of any of this that decides whether a feeling actually reaches someone else, not the decoration sitting on top of it. School, in its standard form, never matched how I think. I do badly with material I have to memorise without understanding why it matters, and well with anything I can build, test, and rebuild. That's a large part of why I'm pursuing Communication Design specifically. it's a field where thinking turns into visible work every term, rather than staying theoretical until an exam decides whether I understood it Currently being signed with a modelling agency in Mumbai, which has put me inside rooms and routines I wouldn't otherwise have access to this early, and given me a real sense of how image and presentation function as an industry rather than just as ideas. What I want from a design education isn't to be taught how to be creative, i presume to already have a hold on that part. I want to be around people who take it seriously enough to argue with me about it, and a structure that turns instinct into a craft I can repeat on demand and maybe eventually for other people whose work needs the same thing mine does, need a way to be felt, not just seen.
